1. What Is This App?

This is our internal tool for tracking everything related to the Community-Based Sensor Network. It replaces the old Salesforce workflow and puts everything in one place, sensors, the communities they're deployed in, contacts at each site, service tickets, collocations, and audits.

Sensor Management

Track every sensor's location, status, install date, and full event history. Move sensors between communities with automatic logging.

Service Tickets

Track repairs and calibrations with QuantAQ. Log each ticket from opening through shipping, repair, progress notes, and return.

Collocations and Audits

Manage collocations and audits. Track progress, upload data, run regression analysis, and generate formatted reports.

Community Tracking

Keep tabs on each community's sensors, contacts, communications, and files. Organized with parent/child community relationships. Optional tagging for sub-networks such as Interior Wildfire Network or Muni of Anchorage Network.

2. Getting Started

To access the app, go to adec-projects.github.io/sensor-network-tracker and sign up or sign in. To maintain site security, the email associated with your account needs to be added to the approved user list first, so check with the team if you can't sign up.

The Layout

The app has a sidebar on the left with everything organized under:

  • Dashboard: Quick overview of the network with summary tiles, sensors needing attention, open tickets, audits in progress, a pod install reference, and a recent activity feed
  • Sensors: Full list of all sensors with search/filter
  • Audits: The audit pipeline (scheduled → in progress → finished, analysis pending → complete)
  • Collocations: The collocation pipeline (in progress → finished, analysis pending → complete)
  • Service Tickets: Track repairs and calibrations with QuantAQ, add progress notes, RMA and shipping numbers, etc.
  • Communities: All deployment communities organized by region, filterable by tags
  • Contacts: Community contacts grouped by location showing primary, active, and inactive statuses for each contact
Tip
You can pin frequently-used communities or sensors to the sidebar for quick access. Just click the star icon on any sensor or community detail page.

3. Dashboard

The Dashboard is the landing page when you open the app. It's a quick-glance overview of the whole network and a starting point for logging.

Summary tiles

Across the top are tiles showing total Sensors, how many are Online, the number of Communities, open Service Tickets, and Active Audits. Click any tile to jump straight to that list.

Needs-attention cards

  • Sensors needing attention: pods carrying an issue status (PM, gaseous, SD card, needs repair, lost connection, or firmware). It shows the top few, with a "+ X more" link that opens the sensor list filtered to just the flagged pods. SD card issues sort to the bottom since they're lower priority.
  • Open service tickets: tickets currently in progress with Quant.
  • Audits in progress: audits that are scheduled or underway.

Pod install reference

A compact, two-column quick-reference of every MOD pod with its current location, install date, and status shown as small colored dots (hover over them to read the status names). Click Enlarge for a full, searchable popup.

Recent activity

Down the right side, a running feed of the latest notes and communications across the network, newest first. Click any item to open it.

Quick logging

The + New Log button at the top lets you log an action from the dashboard without first navigating to the right sensor or community. See Section 6. Logging & Event History for how logging works.

4. Working with Sensors

The All Sensors page is the main view. You can search by sensor ID, filter by status, and click any sensor to see its full detail page.

Quant, Retired, and Other LCS tabs

The All Sensors page has three tabs:

  • Active Quants: the QuantAQ MOD pods currently in use. This is the default view.
  • Retired Quants: MOD pods pulled out of service. Their full history is kept and they can be restored at any time.
  • Other LCS: other low-cost sensors that aren't QuantAQ MODs (for example AQMesh units).

When you click + Add Sensor, you first choose whether you're adding a Quant (MOD) pod or an Other LCS sensor, then fill in the details.

Retiring a sensor

On a sensor's detail page, click Retire Sensor. The popup has an optional "Reason for retiring" box where you can explain why (for example, "Shipped back to Quant after a 4-year deployment"). Type @ to tag a contact. What you write is logged on the sensor's history, and the pod moves to the Retired Quants tab. It isn't deleted, and you can restore it from that tab or from the Trash Bin in Settings.

Sensor statuses

A sensor can carry one or more status tags at the same time (for example "Online" plus "SD Card Issue"). You change a sensor's status in only two ways: in Setup Mode, or through the New Log button using the Status Change action (see Section 6).

Statuses you set

  • Online: deployed and reporting data.
  • Offline: not reporting data.
  • Lost Connection: the pod has stopped reporting.
  • Lab Storage: sitting at a lab, not currently deployed.
  • Needs Repair: has a known problem and shouldn't be deployed until it's fixed.
  • PM Sensor Issue: the particulate matter sensor (OPC or nephelometer) is malfunctioning.
  • Gaseous Sensor Issue: one or more gas sensors (CO, NO, NO2, O3) is malfunctioning.
  • SD Card Issue: the pod's microSD card is faulting.
  • Possible Auto Shutoff Firmware Issue: the pod may be shutting itself off because of a firmware bug.

Statuses the workflows set

These are added and removed automatically as you use the collocation tool, the audit workflow, and Quant service tickets. You can pick them by hand, but it's better to let the tools manage them so the history, dates, and related records stay in sync.

  • Collocation: the pod is part of an active collocation.
  • Auditing a Community: the pod is actively auditing a community.
  • Service at Quant: the pod has arrived at Quant on a service ticket.
  • Quant Ticket in Progress: an open service ticket exists for the pod. Clicking this tag opens the ticket.

Moving a sensor

To move a pod to a different community, use the New Log button and choose Move Sensor. You fill in a plain-language row:

[sensor] removed from [community] and installed in [community]

Pick the sensor and the destination. The "removed from" community fills in automatically to wherever the pod currently is, and the install date updates on its own.

To swap two pods at a site (one leaves for the lab or Quant, another takes its place), click + Add sensor to get a second row and fill in the other pod's move. One log entry then records both moves, and the sensors and communities you choose are added to the log's tags automatically so everything shows up in the right histories.

Sensor Detail Page

Each sensor has tabs for:

  • Event History: Every note, status change, movement, and service event. Filterable by category.
  • Collocations: All collocations this sensor was part of, plus its initial collocation from the original Salesforce data
  • Audits: Any audits involving this sensor
  • Service Tickets: Open and closed tickets with progress notes

5. Communities

Communities are organized alphabetically by parent communities, with child communities nested beneath them. You can change parent/child relationships anytime with the "Change Parent" button in each sensor's details page.

Each community page shows its sensors, contacts, event history, communications, and files. You can add sub-communities, deactivate communities you're no longer working with, and reactivate them later if needed.

How Community History Works
The community event history only shows notes that are explicitly tagged to that community. If a sensor moves to your community, only the movement note (and future notes tagged to your community) will show, not the sensor's entire history from previous communities; that will be visible under the sensor's individual history.

6. Logging & Event History

Everything that happens to a sensor or community gets recorded with the + New Log button, which is on the dashboard and on every sensor, community, and contact page. It opens with a row of action chips. Pick one or more:

  • Call / Email / Site Visit: log a communication with a contact.
  • Sensor Issue: record a problem with a pod (a malfunction, an outage, or troubleshooting you did).
  • Status Change: change a sensor's status. Picking this shows a status list, and the new status is applied to the sensors you tag.
  • Move Sensor: move or swap pods between communities (see Section 4).
  • General Note: anything else.

You can combine chips. Then tag the sensors and communities the log is about, write your note, and type @ to tag any contacts involved. When you save, the entry appears in the history of everything it's tagged to.

Logging a sensor issue

Open New Log, pick Sensor Issue, tag the affected pod, and describe what's wrong and what you did. If the pod should also carry an issue status (PM, gaseous, SD card, lost connection, needs repair, or firmware), add the Status Change chip in the same log and set it. The flagged pod then shows up under "Sensors needing attention" on the dashboard.

Editing a log

Click the pencil icon on any note or communication in the timeline. It reopens the New Log form prefilled with what was originally entered (type, date, text, sensor and community tags, and contact @mentions). Edit anything and save, and the timeline entry updates in place. The action chips are hidden during an edit since those are creation-time actions and shouldn't re-fire on every save.

Adding a Follow-Up to an Existing Note

Each note in the timeline has an Add Note button that lets you append a follow-up directly under that note. Follow-ups are timestamped with your name and date and stay visually grouped with the parent. Type @ inside a follow-up to tag a contact, and they'll be added to the parent note's tags so the follow-up shows up on their contact page too.

Deleting a Note

Click the trash icon. For ordinary notes, you'll get a confirm dialog. For Status Change and Movement notes, the confirm dialog includes an extra checkbox: "Also revert sensor changes." Leaving it checked rolls the sensor's status (or community) back to what it was before the note was created, handy when you log a status change by mistake. Uncheck it if you only want to delete the note record without touching the sensor.

Deletes are soft, the record goes to the Trash Bin (Settings → Trash Bin, admin-only) for 30 days before being auto-purged. See Section 14. Trash Bin & Archived Items.

Event History Filters

On sensor pages, the event history dropdown lets you filter by:

  • Sensor Movement: Installs, removals, relocations
  • Troubleshooting / Site Work: Field work and issue resolution
  • Status Changes: Any status modifications
  • Communications: Logged emails, calls, site visits
  • General Notes: Everything else

7. Service Tickets

Service tickets track the lifecycle of a sensor being sent to QuantAQ for repair or calibration. Each ticket follows this workflow:

Ticket Opened
→
RMA Assigned
→
Shipped to Quant
→
At Quant
→
Shipped from Quant
→
Received

As you advance the ticket through each stage, the sensor's status updates automatically. For example, advancing to "Shipped to Quant" adds that status to the sensor and removes any previous shipping status.

Multi-Sensor Tickets

A single ticket can cover several sensors at once, useful when you ship a batch back to QuantAQ together. Tag every sensor on the ticket and they all advance through the workflow as one unit. Each sensor's individual page links back to the shared ticket, and progress notes appear on all of their histories.

Progress Notes

Each ticket has a Progress Notes section where you can add timestamped updates as things progress. These notes show on both the ticket detail and the sensor page's ticket preview, so you can see the latest status without clicking into the ticket.

Tracking Info

There are fields for shipment tracking info, Tracking Info (to QuantAQ) for the package you ship out, and Return Tracking Info (from QuantAQ) for the package coming back. Just enter the carrier and tracking number, like "UPS, 1234567890".

Closed Tickets

Closed tickets appear in their own section at the bottom of the Service Tickets page

8. Collocations

Collocations are when we place community sensors at a regulatory site (Garden, NCore, or Floyd Dryden) alongside a BAM reference monitor and/or permanent pod to verify the sensors are reading accurately.

Starting a Collocation

Click "+ Start Collocation" from the Collocations page in the sidebar. You'll pick:

  • Location: Regulatory sites are listed first, then all other communities alphabetically
  • Sensors: Tag the sensors being collocated
  • Conducted By: Who's running it
  • Notes: Any details

The sensors automatically get a "Collocation" status tag, and it's removed when the collocation completes.

Collocation Workflow

In Progress
→
Finished, Analysis Pending
→
Complete

Progress Notes

Just like service tickets, collocations have a progress notes section for tracking updates throughout the process. Add notes anytime from the collocation detail view.

9. Audits

Audits are similar to collocations but involve sending an audit pod to a community to run alongside the community's sensor for validation. The audit workflow goes: Scheduled → In Progress → Finished, Analysis Pending → Complete.

Scheduled
→
In Progress
→
Finished, Analysis Pending
→
Complete

Each audit tracks the audit pod, community pod, community, dates, and who installed/removed the equipment. Like collocations, audits have progress notes and a full analysis pipeline.

Updating the Audit Pod's Status

From the audit detail view, click Update Audit Pod Status to change what the audit pod is doing between or during audits. The picker is a multi-select where you can flip the pod between "Auditing a Community," "Offline," and "Lab Storage," and it includes a note textarea with @mention autocomplete for tagging contacts who shipped or received the pod. The note posts to:

  • The audit's progress notes
  • The audit pod sensor's event history
  • Each tagged contact's history

So a single status update gives you a full paper trail without having to log it in three places.

10. Data Analysis & Reports

Once a collocation or audit reaches "Complete," you can upload an Excel data file and the app runs the full regression analysis for you.

Uploading Data

  1. Export hourly data from AirVision for the BAM, permanent pod, and all community pods
  2. When selecting regulatory site data, use PM2.5 and PM10 local conditions
  3. Clean up any invalidated data and trim to the collocation dates
  4. Don't remove the first 24 hours: the app handles that automatically
  5. Define the BAM source (Garden BAM, NCore BAM, or Floyd Dryden BAM) and the permanent pod (optional; if no permanent pod is defined, all Quants will be compared against all other Quants instead of all Quants compared against the one permanent pod. All Quants get compared to the BAM data in both cases).
  6. Upload the Excel file

What You Get

The app automatically:

  • Parses all sensor columns from the spreadsheet
  • Excludes the first 24 hours from regression (the settling-in period)
  • Runs linear regression for each parameter (PM2.5, PM10, CO, NO, NO2, O3)
  • Checks DQI thresholds (R² ≥ 0.70, slope 0.65-1.35, intercept ±5)
  • Generates time series charts and regression scatter plots
  • Runs failsafe validation checks to verify the math is correct

Analysis Tabs

The analysis view has tabs matching our standard report format:

  • Time Series: Hourly plots for each parameter (tabbed by PM2.5, PM10, CO, etc.). Click sensors in the legend to toggle them off/on in the plot.
  • Pods vs BAM: Each sensor compared against the BAM (PM only)
  • Quants PM: Each pod compared against the permanent pod for PM, or each pod compared to each other pod if no permanent pod is defined
  • Quants Gaseous: Each pod compared against the permanent pod for gases (CO, NO, NO2, O3), or each pod compared to each other pod if no permanent pod is defined
  • Data Sheet: Full data table with yellow-shaded first 24 hours and red-highlighted missing/removed data

Saving Reports

Click "Save as HTML" to download a standalone report file. It opens in any browser with all the interactive charts, no server needed. The filename follows the format: Garden_Collocation_Analysis_445_451_674_Mar19_Apr1_2026.html

How Audit Reports Read

  • DQI equation coloring: Slope, intercept, and R² are each colored independently in the equation line: green when the value passes its DQI threshold, orange when it fails. (Colorblind-safe palette.) Plot lines themselves stay neutral so the colors only signal what they need to.
  • Audit dates: Reports use the Actual Start and Actual End fields from the audit, not the scheduled dates, so the header reflects what really happened.
  • Progress note timestamps: Each progress note in the report shows a small timestamp so you can see when it was logged.

Validation Checks

Every analysis includes automated failsafe checks (click the validation dropdown to review):

  • Self-test: Verifies the regression engine is computing correctly using known test data
  • R² cross-check: Independently verifies each R² value using a different math method. The primary method uses least-squares regression and the cross-check uses Pearson correlation; if the two don't match, this is flagged in the validation dropdown on the analysis
  • Timestamp validation: Checks for data gaps, short datasets, and date issues
  • Sample size: Flags if there isn't enough data for reliable results
  • Borderline DQI: Alerts when a pass/fail result is very close to the threshold
Good to Know
X-PM sensors (the Modulair-PM models) only compare PM2.5 and PM10, gas parameters are automatically skipped for these sensors since they don't measure gases.

11. Contacts

Contacts include anyone involved in the sensor network: tribal administrators, school staff, facility managers, etc. They're grouped by community in the Contacts view.

You can tag contacts in notes using @mentions (type @ and start typing their name), tag them in sensor movements, and add them to the email distribution list. Contacts can be deactivated (not deleted) when they're no longer involved, and reactivated later if needed.

12. Exporting Data

Each of the three main lists, Sensors, Communities, and Contacts, has an Export as Spreadsheet button at the top of the page. They all work the same way and open the same field-picker popup.

  1. Click Export as Spreadsheet.
  2. Check or uncheck the fields you want in the spreadsheet. Every field is on by default.
  3. For Contacts and Communities, there's an extra checkbox to include inactive records. Active-only is the default.
  4. Click Download. An .xlsx file named for the list and the current date downloads automatically.

Fields vary by list:

  • Sensors: ID, type, status, community, location, install date, SOA tag, purchase date. Custom sensor fields (added in Setup Mode) are included automatically.
  • Communities: Name, parent community, tags, status, sensor count, sensor IDs, sub-community count, contact count.
  • Contacts: Name, role, community, organization, email, phone, mass-email-list flag, status.

13. Admin Modes (Setup & Sandbox)

Admins see two extra toggles in the sidebar footer: Setup Mode and Sandbox Mode. They look similar but do very different things, so don't confuse them.

Setup Mode

Setup Mode is for bulk data entry and backfilling. Use it when you're correcting or adding a lot of records at once and don't want the app to clutter sensor/community/contact history with an auto-generated note for every single edit.

While Setup Mode is on:

  • Edits still save to the real database the same way, this is not a practice mode.
  • The app stops writing automatic history notes for status changes, community moves, contact edits, etc.
  • The "Date of change" and "Notes" prompts on the status-change and move modals are hidden so you can move through records faster.
  • Extra admin controls appear on the sensors table: add custom fields, edit sensor IDs inline, and restore hidden columns.

Clicking the toggle opens a popup that explains the above. Click "Enter Setup Mode" to confirm. Turn it off again when you're done, normal editing re-enables the automatic history trail. Setup Mode auto-resets when you sign out or close the browser.

Heads Up
If you forget to turn Setup Mode off, your day-to-day edits will stop writing history notes. Watch for the amber "Setup Mode ON" pill in the sidebar.

Sandbox Mode

Sandbox Mode is a safe practice space. You can click around, add/edit/delete sensors, communities, contacts, notes, anything, and nothing you do will save to the real database. Use it to try new features, train yourself on a workflow, or show someone around without any risk.

How it works:

  • You still see the real, current data from Supabase.
  • Any change you make only lives in your browser.
  • Refreshing the page, signing out, or clicking "Exit sandbox" reverts everything instantly.
  • A bright amber banner stays at the top of the screen the whole time so you never forget you're in sandbox.

Clicking the toggle opens a popup that explains the above. Click "Enter Sandbox" to confirm, the page reloads with the sandbox safety net active. Turn it off when you're done, all edits will be automatically saved to the database again. Sandbox Mode auto-resets when you refresh the page, sign out, or close the browser.

Which One Do I Want?
Practicing, exploring, or training? Sandbox Mode. Nothing persists.
Doing a lot of real cleanup/backfill and want to skip the auto-notes? Setup Mode. Everything persists.

14. Trash Bin & Archived Items

Deletes in this app are soft deletes. When you click the trash icon on a note, comm log, service ticket, audit, or collocation, the record disappears from its normal lists but stays in the database, admins can restore it from the Trash Bin in Settings.

Archived sensors live alongside the trash. You can also flip back to them via the Active / Archived tabs at the top of the All Sensors page.

Where to Find It

Settings → Trash Bin (admins only). Filter buttons let you narrow the list by item type: notes, communications, service tickets, audits, collocations, or archived sensors.

Restore vs. Purge

  • Restore: puts the item back exactly where it came from (sensor history, community page, ticket list, etc).
  • Purge: permanently deletes it. There's no undo.

Auto-Purge After 30 Days

Items in the trash bin are automatically purged 30 days after deletion. The window gives you time to recover an accidental delete without letting old junk pile up forever. Archived sensors don't auto-purge: they stay until an admin explicitly removes them, because tickets, audits, and notes can still reference them.

Heads Up
If you soft-delete something and want it gone immediately, click Purge in the Trash Bin instead of waiting for the 30-day cleanup. Purging is permanent.

15. Tips & Good-to-Knows

Timezones
Everything in the app runs on Alaska time (America/Anchorage).
Online & Offline
These two statuses are mutually exclusive everywhere in the app. If you select Online, Offline automatically deselects, and vice versa.
Notes + Status Changes
When you add a note and also change the sensor status, they get combined into one entry in the event history, not two separate notes. The status change text gets appended to your note.
Community History
A community's event history only shows notes explicitly tagged to that community. If a sensor moves in, you won't see its old history from previous communities cluttering things up.
Re-uploading Analysis Data
You can re-upload data anytime from the collocation or audit detail view. Just click "Re-upload Data" and it'll clear the old results and let you start fresh. Take note: the old analysis is gone once you click re-upload, there's no undo.
Deleting Things
When you delete an audit, collocation, or service ticket, all the auto-generated notes in event histories get cleaned up too. You don't need to manually delete those.
